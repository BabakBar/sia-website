# Your Defaults Are Someone Else's Edge Case

- **Status:** outlined
- **Type:** personal technical essay
- **Primary sources:** AutoSpendTracker, FabrikTakt, Taliman-Website,
  LinkedIn/public path: Iran -> Spain -> Mexico -> Germany
- **Length:** ~1,000 words
- **Core claim:** living and working across countries makes software assumptions
  visible: currency, calendar, language, text direction, names, documents, and
  regulatory context.

## Hook

At some point my edge cases stopped feeling like edge cases. They were just my
life: money in three currencies, dates in two calendars, and software that kept
assuming otherwise.

## Evidence

- AutoSpendTracker: multi-currency expense tracking.
- FabrikTakt: Jalali dates and Persian capture.
- Taliman-Website: RTL/LTR bilingual delivery.
- Career path: materials/process engineering roots, UPC, Continental R&D and
  automotive IT/cloud contexts.

## Structure

1. Defaults are invisible until they fail you.
2. Currency assumptions: one account, one country, one reporting unit.
3. Calendar assumptions: Gregorian is not universal in daily operations.
4. Language assumptions: English-first tools miss the real user.
5. Directionality and names: UI/data-model details that become product quality.
6. Lesson for engineers: treat defaults as hypotheses.

## Takeaway

If you have only built for one place, one language, one calendar, and one money
system, you have not seen your product's real boundary yet.
