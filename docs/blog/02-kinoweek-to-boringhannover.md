# From KinoWeek to BoringHannover

- **Status:** outlined
- **Type:** product evolution / build note
- **Primary sources:** KinoWeek, BoringHannover
- **Length:** ~1,000 words
- **Core claim:** the best public products often start as private annoyances with
  a tiny, repeatable workflow.

## Hook

KinoWeek was not supposed to be a product. It was a weekly message so I could
stop checking twelve tabs to find English movies and concerts in Hannover.

## Why this fits me

This story shows the bridge from personal automation to public utility. It also
sets up `boring-network` later without starting with architecture.

## Evidence

- KinoWeek: stateless weekly Telegram digest, plugin-based source registry,
  Astor OV movies, curated concert venues, no database.
- BoringHannover: public site, broader source set, static output, contribution
  path, clear local identity: "Proving Hannover isn't THAT boring."

## Structure

1. The annoyance: local discovery was scattered and noisy.
2. The private tool: weekly Telegram digest with just enough sources.
3. The product turn: when the output becomes useful to more than one person.
4. What stayed the same: stateless runs, source plugins, weekly cadence, signal
   over completeness.
5. What changed: public web layer, source breadth, contribution docs, brand.
6. Lesson: productization is not adding features; it is clarifying the promise.

## Takeaway

If a private automation keeps proving useful, the public product is often just a
cleaner contract and a clearer audience.

## Risks

- Avoid making Hannover jokes feel dismissive; keep the tone affectionate.
